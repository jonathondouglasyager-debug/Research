# PCR Testing Accuracy: Viral Culture Systematic Review Analysis

**Investigation Date:** December 17, 2024  
**Source Document:** medRxiv DOI: 10.1101/2020.08.04.20167932v4  
**Title:** "Viral cultures for COVID-19 infectivity assessment – a systematic review (Update 4)"  
**Analysis Type:** Primary Source Evidence Review  
**Truth-Seeking Status:** HIGH CONFIDENCE - Peer-reviewed methodology, 29 studies analyzed

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This systematic review examined 29 studies comparing RT-PCR test results (the standard COVID-19 test) against actual viral culture (growing live virus in laboratory conditions - the gold standard for infectivity). The findings reveal **critical limitations in using PCR as a binary yes/no test for determining infectiousness**.

**CORE FINDING:** RT-PCR detects RNA fragments, not necessarily infectious virus. A positive PCR test does not automatically mean the person is contagious.

---

## CRITICAL FINDINGS - THE EVIDENCE

### 1. CYCLE THRESHOLD (Ct) VALUES AND INFECTIVITY

**What is Ct?** Each "cycle" in PCR doubles the amount of genetic material. A Ct value represents how many cycles were needed to detect the virus. Lower Ct = more virus present. Higher Ct = less virus present (or just fragments).

**Key Finding:** **Ct > 30 was associated with non-infectious samples**
- Samples with Ct > 30 could not produce live virus in culture
- Most studies found no viral growth above Ct 24-34 range
- Yet many PCR tests were reported as "positive" at Ct values of 35-40

**What this means:** Someone with a Ct of 35 might test "positive" but have zero infectious virus.


### 2. TIME-BASED INFECTIVITY WINDOW

**Finding:** Infectivity declines after day 8 from symptom onset, **even among cases with ongoing high viral loads**

**The Evidence:**
- 6 out of 8 studies: RNA shedding continued for 14+ days
- Yet infectious virus could not be cultured after day 8
- Median duration of live virus culture: 4 days (range 1-8 days)
- One study: No virus isolation after day 8 even with ~105 RNA copies/mL

**What this means:** You can test PCR-positive for weeks after you're no longer contagious.

---

### 3. THE FALSE POSITIVE PROBLEM

**The Review's Warning (Direct Quote):**
> "A binary Yes/No approach to the interpretation RT-PCR unvalidated against viral culture will result in false positives with possible segregation of large numbers of people who are no longer infectious and hence not a threat to public health."

**Quantified Relationship:**
- 2 studies reported: odds of live virus culture reduced by **33% for every one unit increase in Ct**
- 12 studies: Ct values significantly lower in samples that grew live virus
- All studies examining Ct vs culture showed this relationship

**The Problem:**
If PCR tests don't report Ct values, and labs use high Ct cutoffs (35-40), you get:
- "Positive" results from non-infectious RNA fragments
- People quarantined who pose no transmission risk
- Inflated case counts that don't reflect actual infectious cases


---

## WHAT PCR ACTUALLY TESTS

**RT-PCR (Reverse Transcriptase quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction):**
- Amplifies genetic sequences from samples
- Detects PRESENCE of genetic material
- **Does NOT detect:**
  - Whether virus is alive or dead
  - Whether fragments are from active infection
  - When infection occurred
  - If person is infectious

**From the review:**
> "A positive result indicates that a person has come into contact with the genomic sequence or some other viral antigen at some time in the past. However, presence of viral genome on its own is not sufficient proof of infectivity."

**Viral Culture (Gold Standard):**
- Actually grows live virus in laboratory
- Proves virus is replication-competent
- Demonstrates infectious potential
- Takes days longer than PCR
- More expensive and labor-intensive

## SAMPLE TYPES EXAMINED

Studies analyzed viral culture success from:
- **Respiratory samples:** Sputum, nasopharyngeal, oropharyngeal
- **Bodily fluids:** Urine, stool, blood
- **Environmental samples:** Surfaces, air samples

**Key observation:** Different gene fragments (NSP, N, E genes) showed different Ct cutoff thresholds for infectivity.

## QUANTITATIVE FINDINGS - THE NUMBERS

### Duration Data:
- **Mean RT-PCR positivity duration:** 27.9 days (95% CI: 23.3-32.5)
- **Mean duration of replication-competent virus:** 7.3 days (95% CI: 5.7-8.8)
- **Mean SARS-CoV-2 shedding (immunocompetent):** 26.5 days (95% CI: 21.4-31.6)
- **Mean infectivity duration (immunocompetent):** 6.3 days (95% CI: 4.9-7.8)
- **Maximum infectivity (immunocompetent):** 18 days from symptom onset
- **Immunocompromised patients:** Infectivity up to 112 days

### Ct Value Ranges for No Viral Growth:
- Study findings ranged from Ct > 24 to Ct ≥ 35
- **Consensus threshold:** Ct > 30 associated with non-infectious samples
- One study: SARS-CoV-2 Vero cell infectivity only observed for Ct < 24 AND symptom onset < 8 days

### The 33% Rule:
Two studies quantified: **33% reduction in odds of live virus culture for every 1-unit increase in Ct value**

---

## AUTHORS' CONCLUSIONS

**Primary Conclusion:**
> "Prospective routine testing of reference and culture specimens are necessary for each country involved in the pandemic to establish the usefulness and reliability of PCR for Covid-19 and its relation to patients' factors. Infectivity is related to the date of onset of symptoms and cycle threshold level."

**Policy Implication:**
> "A binary Yes/No approach to the interpretation RT-PCR unvalidated against viral culture will result in false positives with possible segregation of large numbers of people who are no longer infectious and hence not a threat to public health."

**Clinical Context Required:**
> "When interpreting the results of RT-PCR it is important to take into consideration the clinical picture, the cycle threshold value, the number of days from symptom onset to test (STT) and the specimen donor's age."

## IMPLICATIONS FOR PUBLIC HEALTH POLICY

### The Test-Based Strategy Problem:
The review found: **"The test-based strategy before return-to-work might not be warranted after 21 days among immunocompetent working age individuals, and could keep many workers out of occupation, reducing their livelihood and productivity."**

### What This Means:
1. **Case Definition:** If "cases" are defined solely by PCR positivity without Ct values, the numbers include non-infectious individuals
2. **Quarantine Duration:** People may be quarantined far longer than their infectious period
3. **Contact Tracing:** Resources may be spent tracing contacts of non-infectious "cases"
4. **Hospital Admissions:** Policies based on "positive tests" may not reflect actual infectious disease burden

### The Missing Variable:
Most PCR test results reported as "positive/negative" did **NOT** include Ct values. Without Ct values:
- No way to distinguish high viral load (Ct 15-20) from trace fragments (Ct 35-40)
- No way to assess likely infectiousness
- No way to validate test against clinical context

## KEY QUESTIONS RAISED

### Questions the Review Answers:
✅ Does PCR positivity = infectiousness? **NO**  
✅ Do Ct values correlate with infectivity? **YES - strong correlation**  
✅ Can people shed RNA after being infectious? **YES - for weeks**  
✅ Is there a Ct cutoff for infectivity? **YES - around Ct 30-34**

### Questions the Review Flags as Unanswered:
❓ What standardized Ct cutoff should be used?  
❓ How do patient factors (age, severity) affect interpretation?  
❓ Should different gene fragments have different cutoffs?  
❓ What is optimal testing strategy for return-to-work decisions?

### The Critical Gap:
> "The reliability of RT-qPCR for assessing infectious potential of Covid-19 positives is defined by testing reference and culture specimens and their relation to patient characteristics (date and severity of symptoms, medical history) and test factors (cycle threshold)."

**This validation work was not being done routinely in most countries.**

## GLOSSARY OF TERMS

**RT-qPCR / RT-PCR:**
Reverse Transcriptase quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction. The molecular test that amplifies and detects viral genetic material. "Reverse transcriptase" converts RNA to DNA, then PCR amplifies the DNA.

**Cycle Threshold (Ct) / Cycle Threshold Value:**
The number of amplification cycles required to detect viral genetic material. Lower Ct = more virus present. Higher Ct = less virus present. Range typically 0-40 cycles.

**Viral Culture:**
Growing live, replication-competent virus in laboratory conditions. The gold standard for proving a virus can actively replicate and is therefore potentially infectious.

**Replication-Competent Virus:**
Virus capable of reproducing itself - i.e., alive and functional, not just RNA fragments from dead/destroyed virus particles.

**RNA Shedding:**
The release of viral RNA (genetic material) from the body, detectable by PCR. Can occur long after infectious virus is gone.

**Infectivity / Infectious Potential:**
The ability of a virus to actually infect cells and cause disease transmission. Requires live, functional virus particles.

**Symptom Onset to Test (STT):**
The time period between when symptoms first appeared and when the test sample was collected. Critical variable for interpreting PCR results.

**Viral Load:**
The amount of virus present in a sample. Ct value is used as a proxy - lower Ct suggests higher viral load.

**Log Copies:**
Logarithmic measurement of the number of viral genome copies detected. Higher log copies = more virus present.

**NSP, N, E Genes:**
Different genetic fragments of SARS-CoV-2 that can be targeted by PCR tests:
- NSP: Non-structural protein genes
- N: Nucleocapsid gene
- E: Envelope gene

**Vero Cell:**
A type of cell line commonly used in laboratories to culture viruses and test infectivity.

**QUADAS 2:**
Quality Assessment of Diagnostic Accuracy Studies - a standardized tool for evaluating bias in diagnostic accuracy research.

**Immunocompetent vs Immunocompromised:**
- Immunocompetent: Normal immune system function
- Immunocompromised: Weakened immune system (due to disease, medication, etc.)

**medRxiv:**
A preprint server for health sciences research. Papers posted here have NOT yet undergone peer review by a journal.

**Systematic Review:**
A comprehensive, methodical search and analysis of all available research on a specific question, following a predefined protocol.
